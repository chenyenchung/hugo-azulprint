---
title: "Preparing for the Symposium"
date: 1951-06-04
summary: "Working out how to present controlling elements to a room that wants a clean story — and bracing for the talk to be heard as 'exception' rather than 'rule.'"
---

The Symposium begins next week. I have been redrafting the talk for most of the
spring, and the problem is not the data. The data are fine. The problem is the
framing.

What I want to say is that the genome is not a fixed library. It contains
elements whose location and activity can change in a single generation, and the
behavior of nearby genes follows. What I am afraid the room will hear is that I
have a curious exception in maize — an oddity, unrepresentative, perhaps
peculiar to the broken ninth chromosome.

The slides I am keeping are the ones with kernels on them. A photograph of a
sectored ear is harder to dismiss than a model. If the audience leaves
remembering anything, I would like it to be the kernels — and the fact that the
pattern in front of them was produced by the genome itself, not imposed on it.

We will see how it goes. I have spent twenty years in maize and I am still
learning how to talk about it.
