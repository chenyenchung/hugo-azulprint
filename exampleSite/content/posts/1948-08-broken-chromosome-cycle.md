---
title: "On the cycle that wouldn't close"
date: 1948-08-21
tags: ["chromosomes"]
summary: "Notes on the breakage-fusion-bridge cycle and the variegated kernels that keep insisting on something more interesting than a steady state."
---

The breakage-fusion-bridge cycle is supposed to settle. A chromosome breaks, the
ends fuse, anaphase pulls the dicentric apart, and after a few generations the
damage is either lost or repaired. Tidy.

Mine are not settling. Plants carrying the broken ninth chromosome keep
producing kernels with sectored phenotypes whose boundaries don't match the
expected mitotic lineage. The variegation pattern is too clean — colorless
sectors with sharp edges, repeated across many ears, and at frequencies that
don't decay the way breakage-loss alone should.

I think there is an element involved that I haven't named. Something at the
breakpoint is being turned on and off in a way that the chromosome itself
doesn't account for. I have spent most of August counting kernels and have
nothing to call it yet, but the pattern is real enough that I have started
designing crosses around it rather than around the chromosome arm.
